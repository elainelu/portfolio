Airbnb UX Redesign

KBCP Design Challenge


This UX redesign was completed as part of the Kleiner Perkins Fellowship application.

This design sprint was completed in one week. During the first few days, I interviewed and surveyed 15 Airbnb customers (20-48 years) to hypothesize improvements for the web experience. Painpoints that surfaced in conversation helped me understand how customers use Airbnb, when and why they use it, and which features are top of mind. The responses can be found here.

Homes, Experiences, Restaurants

I looked to the following three areas of the website: homes, experiences, and restaurants. As it exists, the site operates like a store but I noticed opportunity for the feed and individual listings to faciliatate a more cohesive browsing experience. Visually, the site is content- and filter-saturated, there are few associative entry points from the home feed to the listings pages, and the grid layout could more effectively encourage browsing for combinations (ex: search for experiences and restaurants in Macau).

Opportuntiy Areas

1. Personale browsing. Airbnb provides experiences people love that the website could reflect. Currently, it does not enable people to make discoveries through browsing. There is an opportunity for personalized suggestions that allow people to be inspired by the places they could go and experiences they can have for low prices.

2. Build your wish list. 11/15 people surveyed do not use Wish Lists regularly and 4/15 have never seen the Wish Lists page. Building Wish Lists and Guidebooks have the potential to learn your travel preferences better, and can also serve as a hook to explore Airbnb beyond room bookings.

3. Destination in mind. People do not visit the Airbnb website unless they have a destination in mind. The experience is transactional. Photos show what accomodations look like, but not what they feel like. Potential improvements inlude larger media, videos, and highlights that turn pages into an interactive space.



Next Steps: Airbnb Spaces

There is currently no centralized platform for people to find available open spaces for co-working or hosting activites that feel intimate and comfortable. People use Airbnb to find a place to stay, but everyone should be able to easily book nice spaces for hosting.

Airbnb would gain profits from individual event organizer subscriptions and from selling package deals to local businesses and companies who wish to sponsor their events at local or global conference centers. The eventual goal is to allow hosts and event organizers of any scale to rent out available location properties to host, organize, and promote social activites.
